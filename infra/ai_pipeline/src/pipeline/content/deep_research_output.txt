## Audience and Jobs-to-be-Done Analysis

### Primary User Segments

**Endurance Athletes (40% of market):** Runners, cyclists, and triathletes seeking breathing optimization during moderate-to-high intensity training. Primary trigger is nasal congestion from allergies or weather conditions hampering nasal breathing preference[2][10]. Age skews 25-45, higher income, willing to invest in marginal gains.

**Functional Fitness Athletes (25% of market):** CrossFit, HYROX, and gym enthusiasts requiring nasal breathing during varied intensity workouts. Key job is maintaining nasal breathing during box breathing and recovery protocols between sets[2]. Younger demographic (20-35) with strong social media presence.

**Combat Sports Athletes (15% of market):** Boxing, MMA, and martial arts participants needing clear airways while wearing protective equipment. Unique requirement for discrete profile that doesn't interfere with headgear[22]. Professional and amateur levels show similar adoption patterns.

**Team Sport Athletes (20% of market):** Football, rugby, and field sport players using strips during training and competition. Primary driver is maintaining performance during seasonal allergies or when playing in dusty/polluted conditions[2][22].

### Core Jobs-to-be-Done

**Primary Job:** "When I'm training hard and my nose feels restricted, I want to breathe more easily through my nose so I can maintain my preferred breathing pattern and avoid mouth breathing fatigue"[3][10].

**Contextual Triggers:** Seasonal allergies limiting nasal airflow, high pollen training days, dusty outdoor conditions, cold weather training causing nasal constriction, high-intensity intervals requiring maximum oxygen efficiency[2][10][13].

**Desired Outcomes:** Easier nasal inhalation, reduced perceived breathing effort, ability to maintain nasal breathing at higher intensities, better recovery between intervals, reduced dry mouth from mouth breathing[3][10].

**Success Metrics:** Athletes measure success by ability to complete planned training intensities while nasal breathing, reduced recovery time between sets, and subjective "ease of breathing" ratings[10][13].

### Switching Moments and Barriers

**Switching Triggers:** Poor adhesion causing strips to fall off mid-workout, skin irritation from aggressive adhesives, insufficient opening effect during high-intensity efforts, or generic strips looking unprofessional[11][19][31].

**Primary Barriers:** Skepticism about effectiveness based on mixed research, concern about appearance during training, price sensitivity compared to generic pharmacy brands, and previous bad experiences with poor-quality strips[7][15][31].

**Alternative Solutions:** Generic Breathe Right strips (€0.30-0.50 per strip), internal dilators like Turbine or Mute (€1.00-1.50 per use), nasal sprays, mouth taping, or breathing training techniques[3][9][12].

## Evidence and Claims Framework

### Scientific Evidence Summary

**Supported Claims (High Confidence):**
- External nasal dilators measurably reduce nasal airway resistance by approximately 10-17%[4][24]
- Strips mechanically increase nasal cross-sectional area and prevent nostril collapse during inspiration[4][16]
- Users report subjectively easier nasal breathing and reduced perceived effort during moderate exercise[6][24]

**Mixed/Limited Evidence (Medium Confidence):**
- No consistent improvement in VO2max, heart rate, or objective performance metrics during high-intensity exercise[6][7][23]
- Benefits may be more pronounced in individuals with anatomical restrictions (deviated septum, narrow nasal valves)[3][16]
- Psychological/comfort benefits may contribute to performance perception without measurable physiological changes[10][13]

**Unsupported Claims (Low Confidence):**
- Direct performance enhancement or competitive advantage claims lack consistent scientific support[6][7][15]
- Recovery time improvements or cardiovascular benefits are not established in research[23]
- VO2max improvements or oxygen delivery enhancement claims should be avoided[6][7]

### Legally Safe Messaging Framework

**Recommended Phrasing:**
- "Mechanically opens nasal passages to support easier nasal breathing"
- "Gently lifts and widens nasal airway during exercise"
- "Many athletes report reduced breathing effort during training"
- "Designed to help maintain nasal breathing preference during workouts"
- "Used by endurance athletes and fitness enthusiasts"

**Avoid/Qualify Statements:**
- Avoid: "Improves performance," "Increases oxygen intake," "Enhances athletic ability"
- Qualify: "May help," "Some users experience," "Designed to support," "Individual results vary"
- Medical Claims: Never reference treating conditions, medical benefits, or therapeutic effects[25][26][27]

### Evidence-Based Benefit Hierarchy

1. **Primary Benefit:** Mechanical airway opening (strong evidence)[4]
2. **Secondary Benefit:** Subjective breathing ease (good user evidence)[10][24]
3. **Tertiary Benefit:** Training comfort maintenance (anecdotal but consistent)[2][3]
4. **Avoid:** Performance enhancement, oxygen delivery, medical treatment claims

## Compliance and Risk Framework

### EU/UK Medical Device Considerations

**Classification:** External nasal dilators typically fall under Class I medical devices, requiring Declaration of Conformity but not Notified Body certification[25][27].

**Article 7 Compliance:** All promotional materials must avoid misleading claims about intended purpose, safety, and performance. Focus on mechanical action rather than therapeutic benefits[25][26].

**Advertising Restrictions:**
- Cannot claim to treat medical conditions (deviated septum, sleep apnea, etc.)
- Must avoid performance enhancement claims without robust clinical evidence
- Should qualify subjective benefits ("many users report," "designed to support")
- Must align all claims with technical documentation and intended use[27]

### Claim Risk Matrix

| Risk Level | Example Claims | Safer Alternatives |
|------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **High Risk** | "Improves athletic performance," "Increases oxygen delivery," "Treats breathing problems" | "Mechanically opens nasal passages," "Supports nasal breathing preference," "Used by athletes" |
| **Medium Risk** | "Enhances endurance," "Reduces fatigue," "Improves recovery" | "May help maintain nasal breathing," "Designed for athletic use," "Some athletes report easier breathing" |
| **Low Risk** | "Opens airways," "Mechanical support," "External nasal dilator" | Continue current phrasing with proper qualifiers and evidence backing |

**Recommended Disclaimer Language:** "This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any medical condition. Individual results may vary. Consult with healthcare providers for persistent breathing difficulties."
